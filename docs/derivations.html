Symmetry elements of Platonic solids and Kepler-Poinsot polyhedra

1. Introduction to Regular Polyhedra

A regular polyhedron is a three-dimensional solid whose faces are all congruent regular polygons, with the same number of faces meeting at every vertex. Each is uniquely classified by its Schläfli symbol \(\{p,\,q\}\), where:

  • every face is a regular \(p\)-gon, and
  • exactly \(q\) faces meet at each vertex.

The Angle-Defect Argument

At each vertex of a convex polyhedron, the sum of the face angles must be strictly less than \(2\pi\). The interior angle of a regular \(p\)-gon is:

\[ \alpha \;=\; \frac{(p-2)\,\pi}{p} \]

Since \(q\) such angles meet at each vertex, the convexity constraint reads:

\[ q\,\alpha \;\lt\; 2\pi \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \frac{q\,(p-2)}{p} \;\lt\; 2 \]

The positive difference \(2\pi - q\alpha\) is called the angle defect at the vertex. By Descartes' theorem, the total angle defect summed over all vertices of any convex polyhedron equals \(4\pi\), providing a deep link between local geometry and global topology.

2. Classification Proof

Convex regular polyhedra

Rearranging the constraint above:

\[ \frac{q(p-2)}{p} \lt 2 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; q(p-2) \lt 2p \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; qp - 2q \lt 2p \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; qp - 2q - 2p \lt 0 \]

Adding 4 to both sides:

\[ (p-2)(q-2) \lt 4 \]

Since \(p \ge 3\) and \(q \ge 3\) (every face has at least 3 sides, at least 3 faces meet at a vertex), we enumerate all integer solutions:

\(p\)\(q\)\((p{-}2)(q{-}2)\)SolidSchläfli

No other pairs \((p,q)\) with \(p,q \ge 3\) satisfy \((p{-}2)(q{-}2) \lt 4\). This completes the proof that exactly five convex regular polyhedra exist.

Kepler-Poinsot star polyhedra

If we allow star polygons as faces (Schläfli symbol \(\{p/d\}\), where \(d\) counts how many vertices are skipped when connecting), we obtain four additional regular polyhedra:

3. Symmetry Group Analysis

Tetrahedron — Td (order 24)

The full symmetry group Td has 24 elements. The rotation subgroup T has order 12:

OperationCountDescription
\(E\)1Identity
\(C_3,\, C_3^2\)8Four \(C_3\) axes (vertex → opposite face centre), each with \(C_3\) and \(C_3^2\)
\(C_2\)3Three \(C_2\) axes (edge midpoint → opposite edge midpoint)

Total rotations: \(1 + 8 + 3 = 12\). Adding 6 mirror planes \(\sigma_d\) and 6 improper rotations \(S_4\) doubles the count to 24.

Cube / Octahedron — Oh (order 48)

The cube and octahedron are duals and share the same symmetry group Oh. The rotation subgroup O has order 24:

OperationCountDescription
\(E\)1Identity
\(C_4,\, C_4^3\)6Three \(C_4\) axes through opposite face centres
\(C_4^2 = C_2\)3Same three axes, half-turn
\(C_3,\, C_3^2\)8Four \(C_3\) axes through opposite vertices (body diagonals)
\(C_2'\)6Six \(C_2\) axes through opposite edge midpoints

Total rotations: \(1 + 6 + 3 + 8 + 6 = 24\). The full group Oh adds 3 mirror planes \(\sigma_h\) (parallel to faces), 6 dihedral mirror planes \(\sigma_d\), and the centre of inversion \(i\), giving 48 elements.

Dodecahedron / Icosahedron — Ih (order 120)

The dodecahedron and icosahedron are duals with symmetry group Ih. The rotation subgroup I has order 60:

OperationCountDescription
\(E\)1Identity
\(C_5,\, C_5^2,\, C_5^3,\, C_5^4\)24Six \(C_5\) axes (face centres of dodecahedron / vertices of icosahedron)
\(C_3,\, C_3^2\)20Ten \(C_3\) axes (vertices of dodecahedron / face centres of icosahedron)
\(C_2\)15Fifteen \(C_2\) axes (edge midpoints)

Total rotations: \(1 + 24 + 20 + 15 = 60\). The full Ih includes 15 mirror planes and the centre of inversion, doubling to 120.

Kepler-Poinsot Polyhedra

All four Kepler-Poinsot polyhedra share the icosahedral symmetry group Ih (order 120). This is because they are obtained by stellation or facetting of the regular dodecahedron/icosahedron, processes that preserve the full symmetry of the parent solid.

  • Small stellated dodecahedron \(\{5/2,5\}\) and great dodecahedron \(\{5,5/2\}\) are duals of each other.
  • Great stellated dodecahedron \(\{5/2,3\}\) and great icosahedron \(\{3,5/2\}\) are duals of each other.

Their symmetry operations are identical to those of the dodecahedron/icosahedron listed above.

Star polyhedra: density-modified Euler characteristic

For non-convex regular polyhedra the naïve count \(V - E + F\) no longer equals 2. The correct generalisation uses the density \(d\) of the polyhedron:

\[ V - E + F \;=\; 2d \]

More precisely, using the vertex-figure density \(d_v\) and face density \(d_f\):

\[ d_v\, V \;-\; E \;+\; d_f\, F \;=\; 2D \]

5. Vertex Coordinates

All coordinates are given in a Cartesian frame centred at the origin. We write \(\varphi\) for the golden ratio:

\[ \varphi \;=\; \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \;\approx\; 1.618 \]

and note the useful identities \(\varphi^2 = \varphi + 1\) and \(1/\varphi = \varphi - 1\).

▸ Tetrahedron

4 vertices inscribed in a cube of side 2:

\[ (1,1,1),\quad (1,-1,-1),\quad (-1,1,-1),\quad (-1,-1,1) \]
▸ Cube

8 vertices:

\[ (\pm1,\;\pm1,\;\pm1) \]
▸ Octahedron

6 vertices — all permutations of:

\[ (\pm1,\; 0,\; 0) \]
▸ Dodecahedron

20 vertices in three groups:

\[ (\pm1,\;\pm1,\;\pm1) \]
\[ (0,\;\pm1/\varphi,\;\pm\varphi),\quad (\pm1/\varphi,\;\pm\varphi,\;0),\quad (\pm\varphi,\;0,\;\pm1/\varphi) \]
▸ Icosahedron

12 vertices — cyclic permutations of:

\[ (0,\;\pm1,\;\pm\varphi) \]
